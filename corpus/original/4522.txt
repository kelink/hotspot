Wells Fargo was not starting from scratch in China, said David Hoyt, senior executive vice president and head of Wells Fargo's wholesale banking business.
The China operations were part of a significant platform Wells Fargo inherited from its acquisition of Wachovia Corp two years ago, Hoyt said in an interview in Shanghai.
There has been "a fundamental change" in Wells Fargo's international strategy since the Wachovia acquisition, he said.
Wells Fargo is now the largest trade outsourcing services provider in Asia and is ready to be more active in expanding its global presence, Hoyt said.
The new branch, a Wachovia legacy, will continue to focus on international correspondent banking, he said.
"China is vital to the continued growth of our international business," he said. "Combined with the consistent economic development and the rapid internationalization of the renminbi, we continue to see positive developments and opportunities in China to expand our business and better serve our customers."
Wells Fargo has no plans to form joint ventures with Chinese companies or acquire stakes in Chinese banks, said Richard Yorke, executive vice president and head of Wells Fargo's international business.

Foreign banks accounted for 1.8 percent of China's total banking assets in 2010, according to the China Banking Regulatory Commission.
Profits from foreign lenders in China accounted for 0.9 percent of the country's total in 2010, down from 1 percent in 2009 and 2 percent in 2008.
