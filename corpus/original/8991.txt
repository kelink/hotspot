Critics keen to scupper the two giants' proposed iron ore joint venture include top consumer China, the buyer of more than half all globally traded iron ore, and steelmakers spanning the globe from Japan to Europe.
Stakeholders say a tie-up between the second- and third-largest producers of the raw material used to make steel would be monopolistic, and concentrate the industry, as together Rio, BHP, and Brazil's Vale control about 70 percent of global seaborne iron ore trade.
Here is a timeline tracing hurdles and objections that have dogged the companies' attempts to merge over the last two years.
* Nov. 8, 2007 - BHP, the world's biggest mining group, proposes to buy Rio through a three-for-one share swap. Rio shares soar as much as 30 percent. Rio rejects the proposal as vastly undervaluing the firm and its prospects.

* Nov. 20 - China's steel industry, the world's largest, is deeply concerned about proposed BHP-Rio merger, a Chinese state newspaper reports. Eurofer, lobby group for the European steel industry, says it will ask European Commission to block it.
* Nov. 26 - Rio says it does not need BHP's offer, unveiling plans to double iron ore output by 2018, raise its dividend and generate at least $15 billion in asset sales.
* Nov. 27 - Seven banks pledge to raise $70 billion of financing for BHP's planned bid.
* Dec. 4 - A Chinese newspaper says China's top steelmaker, Baosteel, may bid for Rio, but later the firm denies the report.
* Dec. 7 - Rio says it has had offers from other suitors, but is sticking with its independent growth strategy.
* Dec. 11 - Rio asks Britain's Takeover Panel to set a deadline under its "put up or shut up" rule, under which BHP has to formalise its bid or walk away.
* Dec. 21 - Britain's Takeover Panel imposes Feb. 6 deadline for BHP to make a formal bid, or abandon bid for six months.
* Jan. 25, 2008 - Rio CEO reiterates BHP bid is too low, saying it was "two ballparks away" from fair value.
